A coherent and geophysically credible sequence would unfold as follows. Variations are possible, but this ordering minimizes contradictions.

1. Major tectonic earthquake

Sudden ground shaking affects the town.

Buildings are damaged.

Slopes fail almost immediately, triggering landslides and rockfalls.

Subsurface fractures propagate through bedrock and aquifers.

2. Immediate secondary ground effects

Soil liquefaction occurs in low lying or water saturated areas.

Roads buckle, riverbanks slump, and some wells become cloudy or stop producing.

Groundwater pressure shifts but full well failure is not yet widespread.

3. Offshore seafloor displacement

If the quake occurs at or near a subduction zone, vertical displacement of the seabed happens within seconds.

This initiates tsunami waves traveling outward, unseen at first.

4. Volcanic system destabilization

Stress redistribution from the earthquake alters pressure in a nearby magma chamber.

Within hours to days, an already unstable volcano begins erupting.

Ash and aerosols are injected into the atmosphere, darkening the sky.

Ashfall contaminates surface water and farmland.

5. Tsunami or wave flooding

Minutes to hours after the quake, depending on distance, waves reach the coast.

Coastal flooding occurs.

Saltwater inundates soils, surface water, and shallow aquifers.

Fish die from physical trauma, oxygen depletion, or sudden salinity changes.

6. Hydrological collapse

Days to weeks later, wells go fully dry or unusable.

Causes include fractured aquifers, rerouted groundwater flow, and saltwater intrusion.

Springs disappear or emerge unpredictably.

Rivers may change course or stagnate.

7. Ecological fallout

Fish die-offs expand due to ash runoff, toxic minerals, and altered water chemistry.

Crops fail from ash burial, salinization, or lack of irrigation.

Air quality remains poor, reinforcing the sense of prolonged darkness and instability.

Key realism check

The quake is the trigger, not the sole cause.

The volcano must already be primed to erupt.

The tsunami must originate offshore, even if the town is inland but connected by rivers or lowlands.

Well failure is typically delayed and persistent, not instantaneous.

This sequence is internally consistent and matches observed multi-hazard cascades in tectonically active regions.
